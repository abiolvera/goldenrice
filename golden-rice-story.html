Golden Rice: The Cost of Delay

Abi Olvera · June 2026

Greenpeace's 25-year campaign against Golden Rice—a rice that produces vitamin A, given away for free, approved as safe by four countries—has blocked it from reaching the children it was designed to help.[6][5] Vitamin A deficiency is one of the leading killers of young children worldwide.[2] As of 2026, no country on Earth is growing Golden Rice commercially.

Cumulative, 2006–2024, across eleven countries where vitamin A deficiency, heavy rice diets, and home-grown rice all overlap. The death count hangs mostly on one question — how much does vitamin A really cut child deaths? The charity evaluator GiveWell reads the trial evidence as a 12–24% reduction, so the honest death range is about 138,000 to 276,000. How much usable vitamin A the rice actually delivers is a separate question that could move the number further either way; both are laid out in "What could be different" below.[3][32]

This is a back-of-the-envelope estimate. I'm actively seeking feedback to make this rough estimate better. See below for the full story and calculation, and contact me at abio.substack.com.

What is Golden Rice

In 1999, two professors in Europe created a new kind of rice. They added genes that make the grain produce beta-carotene, the same orange pigment in carrots. Your body turns beta-carotene into vitamin A. They called it Golden Rice because the grains are yellow.[1]

Vitamin A deficiency is one of the leading killers of young children worldwide. The World Health Organization estimates that 250,000 to 500,000 vitamin-A-deficient children go blind every year. Half of them die within 12 months of losing their sight.[2] A 2015 study in The Lancet estimated that about 105,000 children under 5 die each year from diarrhea and measles made worse by vitamin A deficiency.[3]

Even when it doesn't kill, vitamin A deficiency destroys immune systems, stunts growth, and causes blindness. Nearly all of these children live in South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa—places where families are poor and rice is what they eat every day. Regular white rice has zero vitamin A.[4]

The professors essentially made it open-source. They navigated 70 patents held by 32 companies and universities, then struck a deal with Syngenta—one of the world's largest agricultural biotech companies—and set up a humanitarian license: any farmer earning under $10,000 a year can grow Golden Rice for free, save seeds, and replant. No royalties. No surcharges.[5]

Then it got blocked

Greenpeace ran campaigns against Golden Rice in the Philippines, Bangladesh, India, and across Southeast Asia starting in 2001. Their core argument: genetically modified foods are dangerous to the environment and human health.[6] In 2013, activists destroyed a Golden Rice field trial in the Philippines weeks before harvest.[7]

For context, GMOs are broadly used. 96% of soybeans, 96% of cotton, and 92% of corn grown in the United States are genetically modified.[9] Virtually all US sugar beets are GMO. The Hawaiian papaya industry was saved by a GMO variety in the 1990s.[10] Billions of people eat GMO-derived food every day. No major scientific body has found evidence of harm from approved GMO crops.[9]

In June 2016, 107 Nobel Prize laureates signed an open letter calling on Greenpeace to "cease and desist in its campaign against Golden Rice." The letter asked: "How many poor people in the world must die before we consider this a 'crime against humanity'?"[8]

The safety agencies said yes. The courts said no.

Every major food safety regulator that has reviewed Golden Rice has approved it:

  • Australia & New Zealand (Dec 2017)[11]
  • Canada (March 2018)[12]
  • United States FDA (May 2018)[13]
  • Philippines—food safety (Dec 2019), commercial propagation (July 2021)[14]

The Philippines became the first country in the world to approve Golden Rice for commercial planting in July 2021. Farmers started growing it. Then Greenpeace Southeast Asia and MASIPAG petitioned the courts.

In April 2024, the Philippine Court of Appeals revoked the biosafety permit, issued a cease-and-desist order on all cultivation and research, and applied the "precautionary principle"—ruling that the burden of proof falls on those trying to change the status quo.[15] The Philippine Supreme Court case is still pending as of 2026.

In Bangladesh, a regulatory application for commercial propagation has been sitting with the government since November 2017. It has been pending for over 8 years with no decision.[16]

How I got these numbers

I'm only counting countries where three things are true at the same time:

  1. Vitamin A deficiency is a significant cause of child death
  2. Rice is a major part of the diet (not cassava, not maize, not wheat)
  3. The country grows enough of its own rice that switching to Golden Rice varieties would actually reach children

China is excluded because VAD prevalence has dropped to very low levels there. Countries where yams, cassava, or maize are the primary staple (most of Sub-Saharan Africa) are excluded or heavily discounted because Golden Rice only helps if people actually eat rice.

This is a back-of-the-envelope estimate. Not an exhaustive model—a simplified one. I don't have a research team. I'm trying to get the best answer I can to a question no one's been answering. Every input and source is below so you can check the math.

How we calculated "vitamin A delivered per day"

  • Beta-carotene in GR2E grain: 18 µg/g (field-realistic, between lab peak of 31 and Swamy 2019 field mean of 3.57)[17][18]
  • Storage retention: 65% survives 3–6 months of tropical storage[19]
  • Cooking retention: 60% survives cooking[20]
  • Bioconversion: 3.8:1 ratio (Golden Rice–specific; see note below)[21]
  • Child rice intake: 30% of per-capita adult consumption[22]
  • Child RDA: 400 µg retinol activity equivalents (RAE) per day[23]

Important caveat: The 18 µg/g and 65% storage figures are optimistic. The most rigorous field trial data (Swamy 2019) found only 3.57 µg/g in milled GR2E rice,[18] and Schaub 2017 found beta-carotene falls to roughly 13% within 10 weeks of storage before stabilizing.[19] Newer breeding lines (BRRI dhan29) achieve 8.5–12.5 µg/g, and shorter storage would improve retention.[24] With the most conservative published data, all estimates below would drop by roughly 70–85%. The truth is somewhere in between.

Why 3.8:1 bioconversion instead of the WHO 12:1 default: The WHO uses 12:1 as a generic ratio for all plant-source beta-carotene—spinach, carrots, sweet potatoes, etc. But those foods store beta-carotene in green tissue, behind plant cell walls and chloroplasts that the gut has trouble breaking down. Golden Rice is different: the beta-carotene is in the starchy endosperm, not green tissue. The only direct measurement of Golden Rice bioconversion (Tang et al. 2009) found 3.8:1 in healthy adults.[21] A second study in children found an even better 2.3:1, but it was retracted after uproar because researchers didn't disclose that the rice was genetically modified. It is unclear whether this disclosure was necessary. The scientific findings themselves were never challenged—only the lack of disclosure.[39] If someone prefers the WHO generic 12:1 default—which appears to understate bioconversion based on both Golden Rice–specific studies in healthy children and adults—all estimates below would be roughly halved.

Now multiply by years

Golden Rice 2 was scientifically ready by 2005. The improved variety was published in Nature Biotechnology that year, achieving 23x more beta-carotene than the original.[17] The first field trial was in Louisiana in 2004.[33]

But new rice varieties don't get adopted overnight. The famous "Miracle Rice" IR8, released in 1966, went from 3% to 75% adoption in the Philippines over 14 years.[34] That's considered fast. And no variety reaches 100%—even the most successful top out around 70–90%.[34]

So we used a realistic adoption curve: slow at first, then accelerating, then leveling off at a 70% ceiling. We assumed deployment would have started country by country between 2006 and 2013, depending on regulatory capacity and IRRI partnerships.[34]

Under these assumptions, our model estimates approximately 276,000 children's lives lost and 550,000–1,100,000 children blinded across these 11 countries from 2006 to 2024. The blindness estimate uses the WHO ratio: globally, 2–4x as many children go blind from vitamin A deficiency as die from it.[2][35]

Year by year: lives that could have been saved

In the early years, few lives would have been saved because adoption was just starting. By the late 2010s, the model reaches peak impact as adoption approaches its ceiling. The decline after 2021 reflects falling VAD prevalence and child mortality—the underlying problem is slowly getting better even without Golden Rice, due to economic growth and vitamin A supplement programs.

The calculation

Here is exactly how every number was produced, in plain words first.

For each country, each year, the logic is: start with the number of children under 5 who died → take the share of those deaths caused by vitamin A deficiency → subtract the children already protected by existing vitamin A supplement programs → multiply by how many would have adopted Golden Rice that year → multiply by how much of a child's daily vitamin A need the rice actually delivers (with diminishing returns, since the first bit of vitamin A matters most). That gives the lives Golden Rice would have saved in that country, that year.

Then three things are layered on top: (1) every country and every year from 2006 to 2024 is added together for the cumulative total; (2) adoption is not instant — it follows a slow-then-fast-then-leveling S-curve starting from each country's launch year, because that is how new rice varieties actually spread; and (3) the deaths shrink toward the end because vitamin A deficiency has been declining on its own, as incomes rise and supplement programs expand.

The exact formula:

Lives saved (per country, per year) = Under-5 deaths that year × VAD attributable fraction × (1 − effective VAS coverage) × adoption rate × (GR efficacy fraction) ^ 0.60 Where: VAD attributable fraction = P(RR−1) / (1 + P(RR−1)) P = VAD prevalence that year RR = 1.75 (relative risk of death for VAD children) Effective VAS coverage = VAS coverage × 0.70 (VAS only protects ~70% of nominally covered children) GR efficacy = min(RAE delivered / 400, 1.0) RAE delivered = child rice (g) × 18 × 0.65 × 0.60 / 3.8 Adoption rate = logistic S-curve ceiling = 70% × domestic rice fraction × rice-eating VAD fraction midpoint = 8 years after deployment steepness = 0.45 Years of healthy life lost: Deaths: 276,000 × 28 years lost per child death = ~7.7 million Blindness: 550,000–1,100,000 × 21 QALYs per blinded child = ~11.6–23.1 million Combined: ~19–31 million years of healthy life 28 years = WHO avg life expectancy lost per under-5 death 21 QALYs = ~35 remaining years × 0.6 disability weight (WHO blindness) QALY = quality-adjusted life year (a standard health economics measure)

def value_for_year(anchor_points, year):
    """Linear interpolation between anchor years; flat outside the range.
    e.g. if we know deaths in 2020 and 2024, estimate 2022 as the midpoint."""
    years = sorted(anchor_points)
    if year <= years[0]:  return float(anchor_points[years[0]])
    if year >= years[-1]: return float(anchor_points[years[-1]])
    for earlier_year, later_year in zip(years, years[1:]):
        if earlier_year <= year <= later_year:
            how_far_between = (year - earlier_year) / (later_year - earlier_year)
            return anchor_points[earlier_year] + how_far_between * (anchor_points[later_year] - anchor_points[earlier_year])
    return float(anchor_points[years[-1]])

def vitamin_a_delivered_per_day(rice_kg_per_year):
    """µg of vitamin A (RAE) a young child gets per day from Golden Rice."""
    rice_grams_per_person_per_day = rice_kg_per_year * 1000.0 / 365.0   # kg/yr -> g/day (×1000 is kg->g)
    rice_grams_a_child_eats       = rice_grams_per_person_per_day * CHILD_RICE_FRACTION
    beta_carotene_micrograms      = (rice_grams_a_child_eats
                                     * BETA_CAROTENE_UG_PER_G
                                     * STORAGE_RETENTION
                                     * COOKING_RETENTION)
    return beta_carotene_micrograms / BIOCONVERSION

def fraction_of_daily_need_met(rice_kg_per_year):
    """How much of a child's daily vitamin A requirement Golden Rice fills (capped at 100%)."""
    return min(vitamin_a_delivered_per_day(rice_kg_per_year) / CHILD_RDA_UG, 1.0)

def share_of_deaths_caused_by_vad(vad_rate):
    """Of all under-5 deaths, the fraction attributable to vitamin A deficiency.
    Standard epidemiology formula (the 'population attributable fraction')."""
    extra_risk = vad_rate * (RELATIVE_RISK_VAD - 1.0)
    return extra_risk / (1.0 + extra_risk)

def vad_rate_for_year(country, year):
    """Share of children who are vitamin-A-deficient in a given year (declines over time)."""
    rate = country["vad_base"] * ((1.0 - country["vad_decline"]) ** (year - country["vad_year"]))
    return max(rate, 0.02)   # floor: 2% residual in hard-to-reach populations

def adoption_s_curve(years_since_launch):
    """Share of the reachable crop that has switched to Golden Rice, 0..1.
    Slow at first, fast in the middle, leveling off — how new seeds really spread."""
    if years_since_launch <= 0:
        return 0.0
    return 1.0 / (1.0 + math.exp(-ADOPTION_STEEPNESS * (years_since_launch - ADOPTION_MIDPOINT_YEARS)))

What could be different

This is an estimate, not a measurement. Here's where the biggest uncertainties are, and how much they matter:

The beta-carotene content problem

Our model uses 18 µg/g, which reflects optimistic field conditions. The most rigorous published field trial (Swamy et al. 2019) found a mean of only 3.57 µg/g in milled GR2E rice grown in the Philippines.[18] Newer lines bred into the Bangladesh variety BRRI dhan29 achieved 8.5–12.5 µg/g total carotenoids.[24] If you use 3.57 instead of 18, all estimates drop by about 80%. The realistic range is probably 4–12 µg/g.

The storage problem

Beta-carotene breaks down in storage. The most detailed study (Schaub et al. 2017) found that Golden Rice retained about 60% of its beta-carotene after 3 weeks and only 13% after 10 weeks, before leveling off.[19] Our model assumes only 35% loss (65% retention), which assumes shorter storage or improved storage conditions. IRRI is working on low-lipoxygenase lines to reduce this degradation.[19]

The big one: does vitamin A actually save lives?

This is the assumption the death count leans on hardest. For decades the answer seemed settled: giving children vitamin A cut their death rate by about 24%. Then a single huge trial in India (called DEVTA), bigger than all the earlier trials combined, found almost no effect.[37]

The charity evaluator GiveWell dug into this carefully. The honest way to handle one big trial that disagrees is not to use it alone, and not to ignore it — it's to pool it with all the others. Do that and the same body of evidence gives two numbers: a 24% reduction (one standard averaging method) or a 12% reduction (another method that gives the giant trial more weight). GiveWell treats that 12–24% band as the realistic range.[37]

My central number (276,000) uses the 24% end. If the real effect is the 12% end, the death toll is about half — ~138,000. I did not use the headline "4%" from DEVTA on its own, because no single trial should be read in isolation, and GiveWell doesn't either.

One thing this does not change: the blindness number. The DEVTA debate is only about deaths. Vitamin A's role in preventing blindness is direct, physical biology that nobody disputes — so the ~550,000 to 1,100,000 blindness figure stands either way.

How many children die from VAD at all?

Even the total is contested. Stevens et al. (2015) estimated ~105,700 deaths a year.[3] Older work (Black et al. 2008) said 600,000–670,000. The newest Global Burden of Disease figure (2021) is just ~17,000.[38] My model builds its own count from each country's child deaths, VAD rate, and risk, and lands broadly in line with Stevens 2015.

The bioconversion choice

I use 3.8:1 (the Tang 2009 direct measurement on Golden Rice) rather than the WHO generic 12:1, for the reasons given earlier. If you prefer the WHO default, all the death and blindness numbers roughly halve again.

What all this means

Two separate things drive the death range. First, how strongly vitamin A cuts deaths (GiveWell's 12–24%): that alone puts the count at ~138,000 to 276,000. Second, how much usable vitamin A the rice delivers (beta-carotene content, storage, cooking, bioconversion): pessimistic values here could drop it well below 138,000, optimistic values could push it past 276,000. Stack the gloomiest assumptions on both and you get tens of thousands; stack the rosiest and you clear half a million. My headline sits in the sensible middle, and I'd rather show you the whole spread than pretend at a single exact figure.
