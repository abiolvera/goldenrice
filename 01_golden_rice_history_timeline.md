## 1. Scientific Background

Golden Rice is a genetically modified variety of *Oryza sativa* (common rice) engineered to biosynthesize **beta-carotene** (pro-vitamin A) in its edible endosperm — the part of the grain humans eat. Ordinary rice does not accumulate beta-carotene in the endosperm, though the biosynthetic pathway is active in the green leaves.

The purpose of the modification is to address **Vitamin A Deficiency (VAD)**, a major public health problem in regions where rice is a dietary staple. VAD is the leading cause of preventable childhood blindness and significantly increases susceptibility to infectious disease. The WHO estimated approximately 250 million people globally suffer from VAD, including 40% of children under five in the developing world. Annually, VAD contributes to roughly 670,000 deaths in children under five and 500,000 cases of irreversible blindness.

The engineering requires inserting a two-gene construct:
- **PSY (phytoene synthase):** Converts geranylgeranyl-diphosphate (GGPP) to phytoene — the first committed step in the carotenoid pathway.
- **CRTI (bacterial phytoene desaturase):** Introduces four double bonds, converting phytoene through the pathway to beta-carotene.

These two transgenes are sufficient to reconstitute the entire carotenoid biosynthesis pathway in the grain endosperm.

## 2. Development: GR1 (1991–2000)

### Key Scientists

| Researcher | Institution | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Ingo Potrykus** | Swiss Federal Institute of Technology (ETH Zurich), Switzerland | Lead inventor; monocot transformation expertise |
| **Peter Beyer** | University of Freiburg, Germany | Carotenoid biochemistry; pathway engineering |

### Chronology

**1982:** The Rockefeller Foundation initiates a program focused on rice biotechnology to address nutritional deficiency in Asia. Ingo Potrykus begins thinking about grain-based vitamin A solutions.

**1991:** Potrykus formally proposes the concept of a vitamin A-enriched rice variety at scientific meetings. The goal of reconstituting the carotenoid pathway in the endosperm is considered impossible by many fellow scientists.

**1992:** Laboratory experiments begin in earnest at ETH Zurich.

**1993:** Peter Beyer joins the collaboration. The Rockefeller Foundation funds the partnership. Beyer brings specialized knowledge of the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway, extensively characterized in daffodils (*Narcissus pseudonarcissus*) and bacteria.

**1992–1999:** The research program works to introduce and express the multi-gene carotenoid pathway in rice endosperm. The work involves:
- Extracting the *psy* gene from daffodil (*Narcissus pseudonarcissus*)
- Using the bacterial carotene desaturase gene *crtI* from *Pantoea ananatis* (formerly *Erwinia uredovora*)
- Delivery via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation into Kaybonnet rice variety

**1999:** The breakthrough is achieved. Potrykus and Beyer successfully demonstrate that the carotenoid pathway can be reconstituted in rice endosperm — something widely considered impossible. The grains display a characteristic **golden-yellow hue** due to beta-carotene accumulation. Potrykus retires from ETH Zurich in 1999 but remains actively involved in the humanitarian mission.

**2000 (January):** Results published in *Science*: Ye X. et al., "Engineering the Provitamin A (Beta-Carotene) Biosynthetic Pathway into (Carotenoid-Free) Rice Endosperm," *Science* 287:303–305 (January 14, 2000).

**2000 (July):** *Time* magazine features Golden Rice on its cover with the caption: **"This Rice Could Save a Million Kids a Year"** — introducing the crop to global public consciousness.

### GR1 Beta-Carotene Content

The first-generation Golden Rice (GR1) produced a maximum of approximately **1.6 µg/g total carotenoids** in the polished grain under greenhouse conditions. While this proved the concept was sound, scientists recognized it was insufficient to meet daily vitamin A requirements from rice alone. Field trials subsequently showed accumulation approximately **four times higher** than greenhouse conditions, averaging ~6 µg/g — suggesting better growth conditions substantially improved expression.

### Intellectual Property Resolution

A patent analysis conducted in 2000 found that Golden Rice was covered by more than **70 patents** held by various biotechnology companies. Potrykus and Beyer entered negotiations with AstraZeneca (later Syngenta) to resolve IP issues. Within approximately six months, a landmark agreement was reached: Syngenta received commercial rights to the technology in exchange for granting a perpetual, royalty-free license for all humanitarian (non-commercial) use in developing countries. Many of the 70+ patents were also found inapplicable in developing countries where subsistence farmers earn less than $10,000 per year.

The **Golden Rice Humanitarian Board** was established by Potrykus and Beyer to oversee the development of the technology and grant non-commercial licenses to public research institutes worldwide.

## 3. Golden Rice 2 (GR2): The Syngenta Improvement (2004–2005)

### The Bottleneck Identified

Scientists at Syngenta Seeds in Cambridge, UK, identified that the daffodil *psy* gene used in GR1 was the **rate-limiting bottleneck** in carotenoid accumulation. They systematically tested phytoene synthase genes from multiple plant species and identified the **maize (*Zea mays*) *ZmPSY1* gene** as substantially more efficient than the daffodil equivalent.

### GR2 Construction

Syngenta developed GR2 introducing a three-gene cassette:
1. *crtI* (bacterial phytoene desaturase, from *Pantoea ananatis*)
2. *ZmPSY1* (maize phytoene synthase — the key improvement)
3. A non-antibiotic selectable marker gene

All three genes were placed under the control of endosperm-specific promoters. The cassette was introduced into Kaybonnet rice, and researchers selected 23 independent transformation events with between 9 and 37 µg/g total carotenoid.

### GR2 Beta-Carotene Content

| Version | Total Carotenoids | Beta-Carotene (approx.) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| GR1 (1999, greenhouse) | ~1.6 µg/g | ~1.3 µg/g | Original prototype |
| GR1 (field conditions) | ~6 µg/g | ~5 µg/g | ~4x greenhouse level |
| GR2 (2004–05, typical) | 20–30 µg/g | 17–27 µg/g | 80–90% of carotenoids are beta-carotene |
| GR2 (best lines) | up to 37 µg/g | up to 31 µg/g | ~23x more than GR1 greenhouse |

A 50g serving of dry GR2 rice was estimated to provide approximately **60% of a child's recommended daily vitamin A intake**.

### Publication

The GR2 results were published in: Paine J. et al., "Improving the Nutritional Value of Golden Rice through Increased Pro-Vitamin A Content," *Nature Biotechnology* 23:482–487 (2005).

### GR2E Event

From the 23 GR2 transformation events, the specific event designated **GR2E** was selected for further development due to its favorable combination of agronomic performance and nutritional content. GR2E became the regulatory submission event used in all subsequent national food safety applications worldwide.

Syngenta transferred its GR2E lines to IRRI and public sector breeding programs in low- and middle-income countries **free of charge**, in keeping with the humanitarian mission.

## 4. IRRI Involvement and Breeding Program (2001–present)

The **International Rice Research Institute (IRRI)**, headquartered at Los Baños, Philippines, became the central institutional vehicle for developing Golden Rice into farmer-ready varieties for Asia.

### IRRI's Role

**2001:** IRRI becomes the first licensee of Potrykus and Beyer for Golden Rice technology. IRRI agrees to develop Golden Rice as a humanitarian project, distributing it at no additional cost compared to white rice for small farmers and consumers in developing countries.

**2001 onward:** IRRI begins the process of **introgression** — crossing the GR2E transformation event into locally adapted, high-yielding rice varieties suited to conditions in Bangladesh, the Philippines, Indonesia, India, Vietnam, and other target countries. This is critical because the original Kaybonnet variety used in transformation is an American variety not suited to Asian farming conditions.

**2002:** First "regulatory clean" transformation event obtained.

**2003:** A regulatory clean line containing 1.6 µg/g beta-carotene is developed and enters the breeding pipeline.

**Mid-2000s:** IRRI establishes the **Golden Rice Humanitarian Board** network, coordinating national rice research institutes in 16 countries including Bangladesh, China, India, Indonesia, South Africa, the Philippines, and Vietnam.

**2004:** The world's first Golden Rice field trial is conducted at Louisiana State University in Crowley, Louisiana, USA, in September 2004. Potrykus and Beyer are present.

**2007–2010:** IRRI and national partners conduct multi-environment confined field trials across the Philippines and Bangladesh to assess agronomic performance, nutritional content, and environmental safety.

**2010–2015:** IRRI reports ongoing challenges with **yield drag** in some introgressed lines — some Golden Rice varieties underperformed the elite parent varieties on yield. This creates additional breeding work.

**2017:** IRRI publicly acknowledges that Golden Rice is still not ready for release and had not yet been shown to reduce vitamin A deficiency at the population level — attributed primarily to the yield drag issue in introgressed lines.

**2018–2019:** Improved Golden Rice lines with competitive yields are developed. IRRI and PhilRice submit regulatory dossiers for food safety review in the Philippines.

**2019:** Philippines grants food safety approval for GR2E.

**2021:** Philippines issues commercial propagation biosafety permit. PhilRice begins seed multiplication.

**2022:** PhilRice registers the first commercially available Golden Rice variety in the Philippines, branded as **"Malusog Rice"** (Filipino for "healthy"). Limited farmer cultivation commences. PhilRice targets Golden Rice reaching 10% of national rice harvest within 8 years.

**2022–2024:** IRRI continues breeding programs in Bangladesh, India, Indonesia, and Vietnam. IRRI ceases commercial Golden Rice activities in the Philippines following the 2024 Court of Appeals cease-and-desist order.

**Ongoing:** IRRI's Healthier Rice Program is developing stacked varieties combining beta-carotene (Golden Rice trait), high iron, and high zinc into a single variety to address multiple micronutrient deficiencies simultaneously.

## 6. Regulatory Approval History by Country

### 6.1 Types of Approval

Two distinct types of regulatory approval are relevant:

1. **Food Safety Approval:** The authority determines that food derived from GR2E rice is safe for human consumption, animal feed, and processing. This does not authorize commercial cultivation.
2. **Commercial Propagation/Biosafety Permit:** Authorization to commercially grow Golden Rice as a crop. Required for actual farmer adoption. Involves separate assessment of environmental biosafety.

**Date:** December 2017

**Approval type:** Food safety (for human consumption, animal feed, and processing)

**Significance:** First national regulatory authority in the world to grant food safety approval for Golden Rice GR2E. No commercial cultivation in Australia or New Zealand was planned or sought.

### 6.5 Philippines — December 2019 (Food Safety) and July 2021 (Commercial Propagation)

The Philippines represents the most significant — and ultimately most contested — regulatory action globally.

**Phase 1 — Food Safety Approval:**
- **Date:** December 16, 2019
- **Approval:** Philippines Department of Agriculture's Bureau of Plant Industry (BPI) approved GR2E rice as safe for human food, animal feed, and processing.

**Phase 2 — Commercial Propagation Permit:**
- **Date:** July 21, 2021
- **Significance:** The Philippines became the **first country in the world** to issue a biosafety permit authorizing the commercial cultivation of Golden Rice — and the first commercial propagation permit for any genetically engineered rice variety in South or Southeast Asia.

**Phase 3 — Variety Registration and Deployment:**
- **2022:** PhilRice registers the first Golden Rice variety under the brand "Malusog Rice." Seed multiplication and limited farmer distribution begins. PhilRice targets 10% of national rice harvest within 8 years.

**Phase 4 — Legal Reversal:**
- See Section 10 for the subsequent Supreme Court and Court of Appeals rulings that overturned this approval.

### 6.6 Bangladesh — Pending (Application filed 2017)

Bangladesh is the other primary target country alongside the Philippines, given high VAD prevalence (affecting approximately 1 in 5 children aged 6 months to 5 years, and about one quarter of pregnant women).

**Timeline of Bangladesh regulatory process:**

- **2017 (November 26):** BRRI lodges an application to the National Technical Committee on Crop Biotechnology (NTCCB) at the Ministry of Agriculture.
- **2017 (December 4):** Application forwarded to the National Committee on Biosafety (NCB) at the Ministry of Environment.
- **2019 (February):** Bangladesh announces Golden Rice will be released commercially pending final Ministry of Environment approval.
- **2020 (October):** Bangladesh Agriculture Minister publicly acknowledges final clearance is "stuck due to reluctance of the Department of Environment."
- **2022–2025:** Greenpeace and domestic anti-GMO groups intensify pressure following the Philippine legal challenges. The 2024 Philippine Court of Appeals ruling is cited by opponents as grounds for Bangladesh to deny approval.
- **Status as of 2025:** Still under review. No approval granted.

A modeling study estimated Golden Rice could reduce vitamin A deficiency inadequacy by approximately **35% among women and children** in Bangladesh.

## 7. Opposition: Greenpeace Campaigns and NGO Resistance

### Origins of Opposition

Greenpeace launched its campaign against Golden Rice almost immediately after the crop's public debut in 2000, as part of a broader campaign against GMOs in agriculture. Key arguments used against Golden Rice have included:

1. **"Trojan Horse" argument:** Golden Rice is a cynical attempt by the agrochemical industry to win public acceptance for GMOs broadly by associating them with a humanitarian benefit.
2. **Sufficiency arguments:** VAD can be addressed through dietary diversification, supplementation, or food fortification — GMO rice is unnecessary.
3. **Safety claims:** Unsubstantiated claims that Golden Rice poses health or environmental risks.
4. **Efficacy claims:** Arguments that Golden Rice does not deliver enough beta-carotene to make a meaningful difference.
5. **Ecological risk:** Concerns about gene flow to wild rice relatives and disruption of agricultural biodiversity.

### February 2001: The "9-Kilogram" Claim

One of Greenpeace's earliest and most widely cited arguments came in **February 2001**, when the organization claimed a person would need to consume approximately **9 kilograms (20 pounds) of cooked Golden Rice daily** to meet their vitamin A requirement. This claim was based on GR1's low beta-carotene content and overstated consumption requirements. It became a lasting piece of anti-Golden Rice messaging, despite being directly refuted when GR2 demonstrated up to 23-fold higher carotenoid content.

### Vandana Shiva's "Hoax" Campaign

Indian anti-GMO activist Vandana Shiva simultaneously labelled Golden Rice a "hoax" — a characterization repeated in media coverage that reached policymakers across South and Southeast Asia.

### Greenpeace Pressure on Regulatory Bodies and Religious Institutions

Throughout the 2000s and 2010s, Greenpeace actively lobbied regulatory agencies and governments in target countries. In the Philippines, Greenpeace:
- Issued repeated public statements characterizing Golden Rice as unsafe
- Engaged the **Catholic Bishops Conference of the Philippines** to weigh in against the crop
- Filed legal petitions alongside MASIPAG against field trial and commercial propagation permits
- Organized media campaigns framing Filipino children as "guinea pigs" for corporate GMO interests

### MASIPAG and Other Domestic Groups

MASIPAG (Magsasaka at Siyentipiko para sa Pag-unlad ng Agrikultura), a Philippine coalition of farmers and agroecology advocates, became the primary domestic legal challenger to Golden Rice. In 2022, MASIPAG and Greenpeace Southeast Asia filed the petition to the Philippine Supreme Court that ultimately led to the 2024 Court of Appeals ruling revoking the commercial propagation permit.

## 8. The 2013 Field Trial Destruction in the Philippines

### Event Details

On **August 8, 2013**, activists destroyed a Philippine government field trial of Golden Rice in **Pili, Camarines Sur**, in the Bicol region of the Philippines.

The field was part of a series of five trial plots established by IRRI and PhilRice to evaluate:
- The suitability of Golden Rice for Philippine growing conditions
- Farmer-relevant agronomic performance
- Any potential environmental impacts

The trial crop was within weeks of harvest when attacked. Protesters trampled, uprooted, and destroyed the rice within approximately 15 minutes, as documented by GMA News TV Philippines.

### Who Was Responsible?

The attack was carried out by members of **KMB (Kilusang Magbubukid ng Pilipinas — Peasant Movement of the Philippines)** and the group **Sikwal-GMO**. Subsequent investigations identified many participants as **paid activists** rather than independent farmers. The attack was widely misreported in international media — including the New York Times, New Scientist, and BBC News — as having been carried out by "concerned farmers," characterizations later corrected.

Greenpeace's role was indirect: Greenpeace Southeast Asia publicly supported the destruction after the fact, with a campaigner stating that Greenpeace "respects what the protesters did," while officially denying direct organizational involvement.

### Scientific Impact

Ingo Potrykus stated: "Important data were to be collected from that field trial, and this can set us back months." The destruction of near-harvest crops eliminated months of agronomic trial data and required the trial cycle to be repeated.

### International Condemnation

The attack was condemned internationally. A petition circulated by prominent scientists gathered thousands of signatures rapidly. The Philippine government filed criminal charges against organizers.

## 9. Nobel Laureate Response (2016)

### The Letter

On **June 29, 2016**, a group of **107 Nobel Laureates** — subsequently growing to over **150 signatories** — signed an open letter addressed to the leaders of Greenpeace, the United Nations, and governments worldwide, calling on Greenpeace to abandon its campaign against GMOs and Golden Rice specifically.

**Organizer:** Richard Roberts, Chief Scientific Officer of New England Biolabs and co-winner of the 1993 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (with Phillip Sharp).

### Key Language

> "Scientific and regulatory agencies around the world have repeatedly and consistently found crops and foods improved through biotechnology to be as safe as, if not safer than, those derived from any other method of production."

> "Opposition based on emotion and dogma contradicted by data must be stopped. How many poor people in the world must die before we consider this a 'crime against humanity'?"

### Greenpeace's Response

Greenpeace rejected the letter, with campaigner Wilhelmina Pelegrina stating: "Accusations that anyone is blocking genetically engineered 'Golden' rice are false. 'Golden' rice has failed as a solution and isn't currently available for sale, even after more than 20 years of research."

Greenpeace also accused the agrochemical industry of overhyping Golden Rice to gain acceptance for more profitable GMO crops.

### Significance

The Nobel Laureate letter was an unprecedented collective intervention by the global scientific community against a major environmental NGO's policy position. It drew significant media coverage and brought renewed attention to the human cost of Golden Rice delays.

## 10. Philippines Supreme Court and Court of Appeals Rulings (2021–2024)

### Background

Following the July 2021 commercial propagation permit, opposition groups immediately moved to challenge the approval in court.

### The Writ of Kalikasan

The **Writ of Kalikasan** ("writ of nature") is a legal remedy unique to Philippine law, derived from the constitutional right to a healthy and balanced ecology. It can be invoked against acts that cause or threaten to cause large-scale environmental damage and provides an expedited legal remedy for environmental cases.

### April 2023: Philippine Supreme Court Interim Order

In its **April 18, 2023** en banc deliberations, the **Philippine Supreme Court** issued a resolution granting a Writ of Kalikasan to petitioners (MASIPAG, Greenpeace Southeast Asia, and allied groups), ordering government agencies to halt commercial propagation of Golden Rice and Bt eggplant pending full review. In a **June 13, 2023** resolution, the Supreme Court referred the case to the Court of Appeals to hear evidence and render judgment.

### April 17, 2024: Court of Appeals Final Ruling

The Philippine Court of Appeals rendered its judgment:

**What was decided:**
1. The Writ of Kalikasan was granted to the petitioners.
2. A **Writ of Continuing Mandamus** was issued against respondent government agencies, requiring demonstration of compliance with biosafety requirements.
3. The **biosafety permit for commercial propagation of Golden Rice was formally revoked**.
4. PhilRice and IRRI were ordered to **cease and desist** from commercially propagating Golden Rice and from conducting Golden Rice-related activities.
5. The ruling applied equally to **Bt eggplant**.

**Grounds for the ruling:**
The court invoked the **precautionary principle**, stating:

> "By reason of the conflicting scientific views and uncertainties on the risks and effects of Golden Rice and Bt Eggplant, potential severe and grave threats to the welfare of people and the environment arise."

Commercial propagation was prohibited "until such time that the concerned respondent government agencies submit proof of safety and compliance with all legal requirements."

The court's reasoning rested on the absence of **scientific consensus** — noting that both petitioners and respondents presented opposing expert evidence — and concluded that under the Philippine constitution's environmental protection provisions, uncertainty alone justifies precautionary prohibition.

**Who filed the case:**
14 petitioners including Greenpeace Southeast Asia and MASIPAG.

### Parties to the Case

| Party | Role |
|---|---|
| MASIPAG | Lead petitioner |
| Greenpeace Southeast Asia | Petitioner |
| 12 other parties | Petitioners |
| Philippine Department of Agriculture | Respondent |
| Bureau of Plant Industry | Respondent |
| PhilRice (DA-PhilRice) | Respondent (developer/cultivator) |
| University of the Philippines Los Baños (UPLB) | Respondent |
| IRRI | Respondent |

### Reactions

**Greenpeace:** Campaigner Wilhelmina Pelegrina called the ruling "a monumental win for Filipino farmers."

**IRRI and PhilRice:** Stated the crops were safe, citing positive food safety evaluations from Australia, New Zealand, Canada, and the United States, and expressed intent to support an appeal.

**Scientific community:** Expressed concern that the ruling inverted the burden of proof — requiring proponents to prove safety to a standard of "consensus" rather than requiring opponents to demonstrate harm.

### Mid-2024: Appeal to Supreme Court

The Philippine Department of Agriculture filed a petition to the **Supreme Court** seeking review of the Court of Appeals decision. As of early 2025, the appeal is pending.

Under Philippine environmental law, rulings on environmental cases are generally not stayed pending appeal. This means PhilRice and IRRI were required to comply with the cease-and-desist order immediately, even while the appeal is being processed.

### Broader Implications

Legal observers and agricultural scientists noted that the ruling:
- Set a precedent that scientific consensus — not simply reasonable safety demonstration — is required before a GMO crop can be commercially cultivated in the Philippines
- Could inform and embolden opposition in Bangladesh, where the anti-approval campaign cited the Philippine ruling
- Raised questions about whether any GMO crop could satisfy the court's standard in the presence of organized opposition producing conflicting expert testimony

## 11. Estimated Delays and Human Cost

### Duration of Delay

Golden Rice was scientifically demonstrated in **1999**. GR2 lines were ready by **2004–2005**. Animal safety testing showed no health risks by approximately **2002**. The technology was essentially ready for regulatory submission by around **2002–2004**.

The first commercial cultivation approval was not granted until **July 2021** — approximately **17–19 years** after the technology was considered ready. Even that approval was subsequently revoked in 2024. As of early 2025, Golden Rice has not been commercially cultivated at scale anywhere in Asia.

### The Wesseler-Zilberman Study (2014)

The most rigorous published economic analysis of the cost of delay:

**Wesseler, J.H.H. & Zilberman, D. (2014). "The economic power of the Golden Rice opposition." *Environment and Development Economics*, 19(6): 724–742.**

Using a real options model applied to India (where an estimated 78 million children suffer from VAD), the study found:

- The annual "perceived costs" imposed on regulators by opposition had to be at least **US$199 million per year** to explain the observed delay in approval.
- This opposition resulted in approximately **1,424,000 life-years lost** in India alone over the decade 2002–2012.
- The model demonstrated it is economically rational for opposition groups to raise concerns early — before regulators are close to a decision — because the option value of delay is highest at that point.

A 2017 follow-up by the same authors maintained the core conclusion that "misguided regulations in the case of Golden Rice have cost millions of healthy life years and billions of dollars."

### Other Quantitative Estimates

- Harvard geneticist George Church stated in 2016: "A million lives are at stake every year due to vitamin A deficiency, and Golden Rice was basically ready for use in 2002, so it's been thirteen years that it's been ready."
- A 2021 PNAS commentary ("Allow Golden Rice to Save Lives," *PNAS* 118:e2120901118) estimated the annual toll from VAD at approximately **266,000 deaths**, arguing delays imposed by regulatory inaction and political opposition contributed directly to preventable mortality.
- Modeling studies for Indonesia and the Philippines suggested Golden Rice could reduce vitamin A deficiency in those countries by **20–25%**.
- A study specific to Bangladesh found Golden Rice could lower vitamin A inadequacy by approximately **35%** among women and children.

### Caveats

Critics of the delay-cost estimates note the difficulty of establishing a clean counterfactual: Golden Rice required local adaptation breeding even after the base transformation was achieved, and yield drag issues documented by IRRI through 2017 meant the crop was not fully farmer-ready until the late 2010s. Some portion of the delay thus reflects genuine agronomic development work rather than purely regulatory or political obstruction.

Proponents of the delay-cost estimates counter that both the breeding and regulatory processes were themselves slowed by the political climate, and that absent organized opposition, regulatory pathways would have been clearer and faster.

### VAD Status in Target Countries (as of 2024–2025)

VAD prevalence has declined in the Philippines and Bangladesh over the past two decades, in part due to supplementation programs. However, food-insecure populations remain at elevated risk, supplementation programs have incomplete coverage, and economic disruptions can interrupt supplement supply chains. Golden Rice is intended as a complementary "safety net" intervention, not a replacement for supplementation.

## 12. Current Status as of 2024–2025

### Philippines

- Commercial cultivation is **legally blocked** by the April 2024 Court of Appeals ruling revoking the biosafety permit.
- PhilRice and IRRI must cease commercial propagation activities.
- The Department of Agriculture filed an appeal to the Philippine Supreme Court. The appeal was pending as of early 2025.
- Environmental court rulings in the Philippines are not automatically stayed pending appeal; the cease-and-desist order remains operative.
- A planned large-scale randomized controlled trial assessing Golden Rice's impact on vitamin A status in school-aged children (900 children) has been delayed by the legal challenges.

### Bangladesh

- Application for commercial propagation has been pending since November 2017 — over seven years with no resolution.
- The Department of Environment has been the primary source of internal delay.
- Greenpeace and domestic opponents intensified pressure following the 2024 Philippine ruling.
- No approval has been granted as of early 2025.

### India

- India has not filed a regulatory application for commercial cultivation.
- India is part of the Humanitarian Golden Rice Network; field trials have been conducted.
- Approximately 78 million Indian children are estimated to suffer from VAD — the largest national burden globally.
- The domestic GMO regulatory environment remains restrictive following the 2010 moratorium on Bt brinjal.

### Vietnam and Indonesia

- Both countries have conducted field trials under the Humanitarian Network.
- No commercial propagation applications have been filed.
- Both countries are evaluating potential future regulatory submissions.

### Australia, New Zealand, Canada, United States

- Food safety approvals remain valid.
- No commercial cultivation is planned or sought in these countries.
- These approvals serve primarily as safety signals for importing and cultivating countries, and contribute to the scientific regulatory dossier.

### IRRI — Future Direction

IRRI's Healthier Rice Program is developing **stacked varieties** combining beta-carotene (from the Golden Rice trait), high iron, and high zinc into a single variety — with the goal of addressing multiple micronutrient deficiencies affecting over 2 billion people globally with a single intervention.
